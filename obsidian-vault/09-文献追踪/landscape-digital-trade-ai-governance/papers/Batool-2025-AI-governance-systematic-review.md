# AI governance: a systematic literature review

## Abstract

The rapid advancement of Artificial Intelligence (AI) has raised critical governance concerns across multiple levels of society. This paper presents a systematic literature review (SLR) analyzing 28 peer-reviewed articles on AI governance published between 2018 and 2023. Using the PRISMA methodology, we categorize the existing literature along four key dimensions: WHO governs AI, WHAT aspects of AI are governed, WHEN governance interventions occur, and HOW governance is implemented. Our findings reveal that AI governance operates across five distinct levels (team, organizational, industry, national, and international), with a significant "governance implementation gap" where principles abound but actionable mechanisms remain scarce. Transparency and accountability emerge as the most frequently cited governance principles, while mechanisms for enforcement and compliance lag behind. We propose a multi-level AI governance framework and identify critical areas for future research, particularly in the operationalization of governance principles and the coordination across governance levels.

## Keywords

AI governance, systematic literature review, multi-level governance, governance principles, governance mechanisms

## 1. Introduction

Artificial Intelligence (AI) systems are increasingly integrated into critical decision-making processes across healthcare, finance, transportation, and public administration. The governance of these systems—encompassing the norms, rules, and institutions that shape their development and deployment—has become a central concern for researchers, policymakers, and practitioners alike. Despite growing attention, the field of AI governance lacks a unified theoretical framework, leading to fragmented approaches that are difficult to compare and integrate.

## 2. Research Method

We conducted a systematic literature review following the PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses) guidelines. The search was conducted across Web of Science and Scopus databases, covering publications from January 2018 to December 2023. Initial search yielded 1,247 results, which were screened down to 28 articles meeting our inclusion criteria: (1) published in peer-reviewed journals, (2) explicitly addressing AI governance, (3) written in English, and (4) presenting original research (not mere commentary).

## 3. Key Findings

### 3.1 WHO Governs AI
AI governance involves multiple actors across five levels:
- **Team level**: Data scientists, ML engineers, project managers
- **Organizational level**: CTOs, ethics boards, compliance officers
- **Industry level**: Standards bodies, industry consortia
- **National level**: Government agencies, regulators, legislatures
- **International level**: OECD, EU, UN, ISO

### 3.2 WHAT is Governed
The literature addresses governance of: AI systems and algorithms, data quality and bias, accountability and liability, transparency and explainability, privacy and security, social and economic impact.

### 3.3 WHEN Governance Occurs
Governance interventions span the entire AI lifecycle: design and development, testing and validation, deployment and monitoring, retirement and decommissioning. A shift from post-hoc accountability to prospective governance is observed.

### 3.4 HOW Governance is Implemented
Governance mechanisms include: hard regulation (legislation, standards), soft regulation (guidelines, principles, self-regulation), technical tools (algorithmic audits, impact assessments), organizational structures (ethics boards, review processes).

## 4. Governance Implementation Gap

A critical finding is the "governance implementation gap": while numerous principles and frameworks have been proposed (e.g., OECD AI Principles, EU Ethics Guidelines), there is limited empirical evidence on their operationalization and effectiveness. The gap between "principles" and "practice" represents the most significant challenge in the field.

## 5. Proposed Framework

We propose a multi-level AI governance framework that integrates all four dimensions (WHO-WHAT-WHEN-HOW) and identifies coordination mechanisms between governance levels.

## 6. Limitations

- Limited to WOS/Scopus indexed journals; policy reports (OECD, WTO) not covered
- SLR cutoff December 2023; does not include EU AI Act finalization and WTO JSI developments
- English-language publications only

## 7. Future Research Directions

1. Empirical studies on governance principle operationalization
2. Cross-level governance coordination mechanisms
3. Governance effectiveness measurement
4. Inclusion of non-Western governance perspectives
