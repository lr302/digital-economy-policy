# Comparing Apples to Oranges: A Taxonomy for Navigating the Global Landscape of AI Regulation

## Abstract

The global landscape of AI regulation is rapidly evolving, with multiple jurisdictions proposing and enacting AI governance frameworks. However, comparing these frameworks is challenging due to fundamental differences in their approaches, scope, and underlying philosophies. This paper proposes a systematic taxonomy for comparing AI regulatory regimes across six dimensions: (1) technology-oriented vs. application-oriented regulation, (2) horizontal vs. vertical regulatory structure, (3) ex-ante vs. ex-post intervention timing, (4) digital legal maturity, (5) enforcement mechanisms, and (6) stakeholder participation. We apply this taxonomy to five "early mover" jurisdictions: the EU AI Act, US Executive Order 14110, Canada's AIDA, China's Interim Measures for Generative AI Services, and Brazil's AI Bill. Our analysis reveals that these frameworks differ not only in their substantive requirements but in their fundamental regulatory philosophies, creating risks of regulatory fragmentation that could impede AI interoperability and international trade.

## Taxonomy Dimensions

### Dimension 1: Regulatory Orientation
- **Technology-oriented**: Regulate AI as a technology class (e.g., EU AI Act)
- **Application-oriented**: Regulate AI use in specific sectors (e.g., US sectoral approach)

### Dimension 2: Regulatory Structure
- **Horizontal**: Single unified AI law covering all applications (EU, Canada, Brazil, China)
- **Vertical**: Sector-specific AI rules (US via FDA, FTC, DOT, etc.)

### Dimension 3: Intervention Timing
- **Ex-ante**: Pre-deployment compliance assessment (EU conformity assessment, China algorithm registration)
- **Ex-post**: Post-deployment accountability and liability (US agency enforcement)

### Dimension 4: Digital Legal Maturity
Measures the existing digital legal infrastructure that AI regulation builds upon:
- EU: GDPR, Digital Services Act, Digital Markets Act
- US: Section 230, FTC Act, sectoral privacy laws
- China: Cybersecurity Law, Data Security Law, PIPL
- Canada: PIPEDA, proposed Consumer Privacy Protection Act
- Brazil: LGPD

### Dimension 5: Enforcement Mechanisms
- **Certification-based**: CE marking style (EU)
- **Agency-based**: FTC, FDA enforcement (US)
- **Administrative**: CAC/MIIT enforcement (China)
- **Ombudsman-based**: Privacy Commissioner (Canada)
- **New agency**: Dedicated AI regulatory authority (Brazil)

### Dimension 6: Stakeholder Participation
- **High**: Multiple rounds of public consultation (EU, Canada)
- **Medium**: Industry-led standards processes (US, Brazil)
- **Low**: Limited public consultation (China)

## Key Insights

1. **Regulatory fragmentation risk**: Different approaches may create de facto trade barriers for AI systems operating across jurisdictions
2. **No "one-size-fits-all"**: Each approach reflects distinct legal traditions, economic priorities, and political systems
3. **Convergence in principles, divergence in implementation**: Transparency, accountability, and safety are universally cited, but operationalized very differently
4. **The "Brussels Effect"**: The EU's horizontal, risk-based approach is influencing other jurisdictions (e.g., Brazil's bill closely mirrors EU AI Act)

## Limitations

- Analysis limited to five jurisdictions; does not cover India, Japan, South Korea, or ASEAN
- Rapidly evolving regulatory landscape; some provisions may change before publication
- Focus on national-level regulation; regional frameworks (AU AI Convention, ASEAN AI Guide) not covered
