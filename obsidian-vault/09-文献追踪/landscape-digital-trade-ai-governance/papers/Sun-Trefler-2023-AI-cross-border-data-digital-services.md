# The Impact of AI and Cross-Border Data Regulation on International Trade in Digital Services

## Abstract

We study how AI algorithms and cross-border data flow restrictions jointly affect international trade in digital services. We incorporate AI into a multi-country Melitz (2003) model of trade with heterogeneous firms. AI reduces the matching cost between service providers and foreign clients, thereby increasing trade. However, data localization requirements raise the cost of AI provision by restricting cross-border data flows, partially offsetting AI's trade-promoting effects. Using data on digital service exports from 42 countries (2015-2020), we find that: (1) a 10% increase in AI penetration raises digital service exports by 6.2%; (2) data localization requirements reduce digital service exports by 18.7%, with the effect being significantly stronger in AI-intensive sectors; (3) the policy simulation suggests that a WTO-wide data free flow provision would increase global digital service trade by $312 billion annually. Our results highlight the critical interdependence between AI adoption and cross-border data governance.

## Key Findings

1. **AI penetration effect**: A 10% increase in AI adoption leads to a 6.2% increase in digital service exports, primarily through reduced matching costs between foreign buyers and domestic providers.

2. **Data localization offset**: Data localization requirements reduce digital service exports by 18.7%. This effect is approximately 40% larger in AI-intensive sectors, suggesting that data restrictions disproportionately harm the sectors that benefit most from AI.

3. **Policy simulation**: Implementing a WTO-wide cross-border data flow provision (similar to CPTPP Article 14.11) would increase global digital service trade by an estimated $312 billion per year.

4. **Heterogeneity**: The effects are stronger for services with higher information asymmetry (finance, consulting, education) and for exports to countries with more stringent data regulations.

## Methodology

- **Theoretical model**: Extended Melitz (2003) with AI-augmented matching function
- **Data**: Digital service trade flows, 42 countries, 2015-2020
- **AI penetration**: Measured via firm-level survey data on AI adoption
- **Data localization index**: Constructed from national regulatory databases

## Policy Implications

1. Digital trade rules should distinguish between personal data and non-personal data, with fewer restrictions on the latter
2. International coordination on data governance is essential to avoid regulatory arbitrage
3. Developing countries may face disproportionate costs from data localization if they are net importers of AI-intensive services

## Limitations

- AI penetration data from surveys may contain measurement error
- Data localization index does not distinguish personal vs. commercial data
- Sample limited to 42 countries; coverage of developing economies incomplete
- Pre-generative AI era data (pre-2023); effects of ChatGPT-class AI not captured
